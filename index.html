Innocence Lost: Understanding the Impact of War Conflict and Terrorism on the Youngest Lives

Children can unfortunately be among the most vulnerable and affected populations in times of conflict and war. War crimes, which are serious violations of international humanitarian law, can have devastating consequences for civilians, including children. Children may suffer directly from violence, forced displacement, and loss of family members, and they may also experience long-term physical and psychological trauma. There are instances where children are specifically targeted or unintentionally harmed as a result of war crimes. This includes acts such as the recruitment of child soldiers, deliberate attacks on schools and hospitals, and the use of indiscriminate weapons in populated areas. Various international organizations and conventions, such as the United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child, aim to protect children during armed conflicts and ensure their rights are upheld. However, the reality is that in many conflicts, children continue to be deeply affected by the consequences of war crimes. The visualization presented above highlights the top ten countries with the highest death rates for both females and males due to war conflicts. This data underscores the disproportionate impact of war on men, as evident in the stark difference between male and female death rates. For instance, in Syria in 2015, the male death rate was 310.0%, while the female death rate was 147.1%. While men bear the brunt of direct war casualties, women face disproportionate and often overlooked hardships throughout and beyond the battlefield. This gender disparity stems from various factors, including the targeted recruitment of men into the armed forces and their subsequent exposure to direct combat. Consequently, men experience higher fatality rates in the top ten countries mentioned. However, women's suffering extends far beyond immediate combat casualties. They endure a multitude of challenges, including sexual and gender-based violence, limited access to essential resources, and increased caregiving burdens. These hardships often persist long after the cessation of hostilities, leaving women grappling with long-lasting physical, psychological, and socioeconomic consequences.
In a report called "The Gendered Impact of War" by the United Nations Office for Disarmament Affairs comprehensively examines the distinct and often overlooked effects of war on women and girls. It emphasizes that while all individuals are susceptible to the devastating consequences of armed conflict, women and girls face unique vulnerabilities and heightened risks.

The report highlights several key aspects of the gendered impact of war:

1. Differential Exposure to Violence: Women and girls are disproportionately affected by sexual and gender-based violence (SGBV) during and after armed conflicts. They face a higher risk of rape, sexual assault, exploitation, and trafficking.

2. Limited Access to Resources: War disrupts essential services and infrastructure, leaving women and girls with limited access to healthcare, education, and economic opportunities. This lack of access exacerbates their vulnerability and hinders their ability to recover from the conflict.

3. Increased Caregiving Burdens: Women often assume primary responsibility for childcare and care of the elderly in conflict zones. This burden intensifies during times of war, as traditional support systems may be disrupted or absent.

4. Psychosocial Impacts: The psychological trauma of war profoundly affects women and girls, contributing to anxiety, depression, and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). The stigma associated with SGBV further isolates and traumatizes women and girls.

5. Underrepresentation in Peace Processes: Women's voices and perspectives are often marginalized in peace negotiations and post-conflict reconstruction efforts. This lack of representation hinders the development of inclusive and sustainable peace solutions that address the specific needs of women and girls.

The importance of gender-sensitive approaches to disarmament, conflict resolution, and peace-building is emphasized in the report's conclusion. In order to guarantee that women's needs and concerns are sufficiently addressed, it demands that women participate fully and effectively in all facets of peace processes. Our best chance of achieving a more just and equitable peace is to acknowledge and address the gendered aspects of warfare.

Between 2010 and 2019, there was a different pattern in the Middle East landscape for the child death rate in armed conflicts: in some countries, the death rate of female children increased relative to the rate of male children. This trend creates questions about the accepted belief that children of color are particularly impacted by war-related deaths.

The visual below reveals notable instances where female child mortality surpassed that of male children.
In 2016, Afghanistan witnessed a stark reversal of this trend, with girls aged 0-14 experiencing a death rate of 40.3%, exceeding the boys' death rate of 35.1%. This deviation from the norm highlights the complexities of child mortality in conflict zones, where factors beyond gender can play a significant role.

Syria, another country grappling with the consequences of armed conflict, also displayed an unexpected trend in 2016. While the overall death rate for both genders was alarmingly high, female child mortality reached 137.7%, surpassing the boys' death rate of 122.4%. This disparity suggests that girls in Syria may face unique vulnerabilities during times of conflict.

The trend of higher female child mortality is not limited to these two countries. In Palestine, for instance, the death rate for girls aged 0-14 stood at 5.3% in 2016, compared to 4.1% for boys. While the difference is narrower, it still highlights the potential for female children to be disproportionately affected by war-related violence.


The above visuals effectively represent the complex and multifaceted effects of war and armed conflict on various demographic groups. Although the clear gender differences in adult deaths highlight how specifically men are targeted by war, a more nuanced story is revealed when focusing on children under the age of 14.

The current trend of higher male child mortality during conflicts is consistent with historical trends, where boys are frequently targeted because of a variety of factors, such as direct exposure to combat and conscription practices. But our investigation also turned up cases where female child mortality exceeded that of male child mortality, highlighting the complex issues that children in conflict zones face.

In conclusion, yes Children do pay the price of War with their lives, there is a clear and distressing connection between the causes of death in wars and terrorism and the ages of the people affected, particularly children who are often deliberately targeted. A 2019 UNICEF report revealed a 20% increase in the number of children killed or maimed in armed conflicts in 2018, emphasizing their vulnerability. Children are increasingly used as soldiers and face a higher risk of being killed by explosive weapons.

Various factors contribute to children's vulnerability, including being caught in crossfire, targeted by armed groups, and the deliberate destruction of schools and hospitals. The use of explosive weapons in populated areas and the enlistment of children as soldiers exacerbate the tragedy. Despite international condemnation, the use of children in armed conflicts persists globally.

The impact of armed conflicts on children is a profound tragedy, and it is crucial to recognize them as individuals with families whose lives have been unjustly taken. Efforts to prevent war crimes and protect children involve diplomatic initiatives, international cooperation, and accountability mechanisms. Upholding the rights of children, as outlined in the United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child, is fundamental to safeguarding their well-being amidst the challenges of armed conflicts. A comprehensive approach that addresses immediate and long-term needs, including education, healthcare, and psychosocial support, is essential for building a safer world for children caught in the crossfire.
